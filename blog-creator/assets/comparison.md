# Comparison

> When to use: "[X] vs [Y]", product/tool/method comparisons, buying guides
> Minimum length: 1,500 words

## Bones (required)

1. **Quick comparison table** — appears early. Covers the criteria that matter most. Works for 2 options or 5 — adapt the table to the actual number being compared.
2. **Criterion-by-criterion analysis** — each criterion gets its own section with evidence and a clear winner (or honest tie). The number of criteria should match what actually differentiates the options.
3. **Verdict** — a clear recommendation with situational guidance ("Choose A if... Choose B if..."). Don't be wishy-washy.

## Moves (pick 1-3 as relevant)

- **Brief overview of each option** — useful when readers might not know one of the options well
- **Pricing breakdown** — when cost is a real differentiator, not just a checkbox
- **Migration / switching guide** — when readers might be moving from one to the other
- **FAQ** — common questions about the comparison
- **Use case scenarios** — "If you're a [persona], here's what we'd pick"
- **TL;DR** — one-paragraph verdict at the top for readers who just want the answer

## Guidance

- Treat all options fairly. Present evidence, not preference. But do commit to a verdict — readers came here for a decision.
- The comparison table is the most valuable element. Invest time making it accurate and scannable.
- Some criteria matter more than others. Lead with the decisive ones, not alphabetical order.

## Anti-patterns

- Don't assume exactly 2 options. The format works for "A vs B vs C" just as well.
- Don't add criteria just to fill the table. If two products are identical on a dimension, skip it or note the tie briefly.
- Don't hedge the verdict with "it depends on your needs" without specifying which needs point where.
